A Journey Through Web History

USSR Launches Sputnik

USSR launches Sputnik into space and with it, global communications. This was a big event in the Cold War, as well as a breakthrough in technology and communication, which allowed the Soviet Union to be alert at all times.

1957

Bell Laboratories Invents Modem

Bell Laboratories researchers invent the modem, which converts digital signals to electrical (analog) signals and back, enabling communication between computers.

1958

U.S Government Creates ARPA

The United States Government creates the Advanced Research Projects Agency (ARPA) in response to the Sputnik Launch. Their task was to invent new technologies to rival the Soviet Union. They would later be renamed to the Defence Advanced Research Projects Agency (DARPA).

1958

ASCII is Developed

The first universal standard for computers, ASCII (American Standard Code for Informational Exchange) is developed by a joint industry-government commitee. ASCII permits machines from different manufacturers to exchange data, with a character-encoding scheme. It would eventually become the standard character-encoding scheme for the WWW.

1963

Paul Baran and the packet switched network design

After joining the RAND Corporation, Paul Baran took on the task of designing a "survivable" communications system that could maintain communications between end points in the face of damage from nuclear weapons. The technology revolutionizes data communications.

1965

First Wide-area Network

Lawrence Roberts (MIT) and Thomas Marill got an ARPA contract to create the first wide-area network (WAN) connecting via long distant dial-up between a TX-2 in Massachusetts and a Q-32 in California. The system confirms that packet switching offers the most promising model for communication between computers.

1965

ARPAnet Project Initiated

ARPA started developing the idea of an inter-networked set of terminals to share cumputing resources, the reference materials which they considered included Baran and the RAND Corporation's "On Distrubuted Communications" volumes. The resiliency of a packet-switched network that uses link-state routing protocols used on the Internet stems in some part from the research to develop a network that could survive a nuclear attack. ARPAnet was one of the most significant predecessors to the WWW.

1966

UCLA Team Sends First Data Packets

The first data packets are sent between networked computers on October 29th by Charley Kline at UCLA, under supervision of Professor Leonard Kleinrock. The first attempt resulted in the system crashing as the letter G of “Login” was entered. The second attempt was successful.

1969

Peter Kirstein Starts European ARPAnet

Professor Peter Kirstein of Univeristy College London starts the first European ARPAnet node with transatlantic IP connectivity.

1970

Ray Tomlinson Invents Email

Ray Tomlinson of BBN invents an email program to send messages across a distributed network. The "@" sign is chosen from the punctuation keys on Tomlinson's Model 33 Teletype to separate local from global emails, making "user@host" the email standard.

1970

Robert Kahn Demonstrates ARPAnet to Public

Robert Kahn demonstrates the ARPAnet to the public for the first time by connecting 20 different computers at the International Computer Communication Conference, and in doing so, imparts the importance of packet-switching technology.

1972

TCP/IP Protocol Development Begins

Development begins on what will eventually be called TCP/IP protocol by a group headed by Vint Cerf (Stanford) and Robert Kahn (DARPA). The new protocol will allow diverse computer networks to interconnect and communicate with each other.

1973

University College of London establishes the first international ARPAnet link

The first international connection to the ARPAnet is made by University College of London (England) via NORSAR (Norway).

1973

Vint Cerf, Robert Kahn coin 'Internet'

Vint Cerf and Robert Kahn publish "A Protocol for Packet Network Interconnection" which specifies in detail the design of a Transmission Control Program (TCP) and coins the term “Internet” for the first time.

1974

Bolt Beranek and Newman founds Telenet

Lawrence Roberts helps Bolt Beranek and Newman (BBN) found Telenet, the first public packet data service, a commercial version of ARPAnet.

1974

First spam ever sent

Gary Thuerk, a marketer for the Digital Equipment Corporation, blasted out his message to 400 of the 2600 people on ARPAnet.

1978

Lawrence Landweber Creates CSNET

Lawrence Landweber creates CSNET (Computer Science Network), a network for all US university and industrial computer research groups. By 1984, over 180 university, industrial and government computer science departments are participating in CSNET.

1980

First Public WAN Initiated

Teus Hagen initiates the European Unix Network (EUnet) as the EUUG dial-up service, which becomes the first public wide area network in Europe, serving four initial "backbones".

1982

Tim Berners-Lee invents the Web

The world wide web is created by Tim Berners-Lee at CERN, the European Laboratory for Particle Physics, in Switzerland. This was a result of his work with the project ENQUIRE. The Web is released with HTML as its publishing language.

1989

The First Web Browser

The first web browser is created by Sir Tim Berners-Lee in 1990. It was simply called WorldWideWeb at first, but later dubbed Nexus. At the time, it was only a way of navigating websites, and nothing like the browsers we use today. However, it was the groundwork for internet browsing and one of the main reasons Tim Berners-Lee was knighted.

1990

CERN's website

The first website ever, CERN’s website, was put online. With their website, they were able to share information among their researchers.

1991

Erwise Released

Erwise was released to the public, and it was the first web browser with a graphical interface. It was developed by four Finish students from a suggestion by Robert Cailliau, and later encouraged by Sir Tim Berners-Lee. Even though the project itself was discontinued, it was a big breakthrough for web browsing.

1992

Mosaic Released

The first “popular” browser was released, called Mosaic. This browser was developed by Marc Andreessen, and it was the first browser that made things easily accessible to the public. This led to an explosion in web activity, and a massive success. However, Marc Andreessen ended up forming his own company, Netscape, in 1994.

1993

W3C forms

In October 1994, W3C (World Wide Web Consortium) is formed at Massachusetts Institute of Technology, in collaboration with CERN. Tim Berners-Lee is the founder. W3C was also hosted by the Keio University of Japan, and INRIA (Institut National de Recherche en Informatique et Automatique).

1994

Yahoo!

Yahoo! is founded by Jerry Yang and David Filo. However, the website was called “Jerry and David’s guide to the World Wide Web,” until 1995. Yahoo! is an entire Internet corporation, and is mostly known for its search-engine, web portal and mail. After Google, it is the most popular search-engine, with more than half a billion visitors every month.

1994

First Delivery!

A large pepperoni, mushroom and extra cheese pizza from Pizza Hut serves as the first online order ever made. This would later lead to a big success for the company, and revolutionized the way people buy food.

1994

Netscape Navigator!

Marc Andreessen’s company Netscape is created, and Netscape Navigator is released. This browser was heavily influenced by Mosaic, but it surpassed it, and quickly became the world’s most popular browser. However, it didn’t take long before Microsoft responded with their own product in 1995, which would become even more successful.

1994

Amazon

Amazon.com is founded by Jeff Bezos, first under the name "Cadabra". Amazon started as an online bookstore, but it soon expanded into selling DVD`s, games, clothes and all kinds of products. Today it is the largest internet based company in the United States, and it also branches out into other regions, with alternate sites such as Amazon.co.uk.

1994

Microsoft Strikes Back!

Internet Explorer (IE) is released by Microsoft, in 1995. At the time, IE was a huge breakthrough. This was mainly because it was bundled with every computer from Microsoft. Following its release, IE and Netscape Navigator clashed together in the first browser war, with Explorer coming out on top. IE is most commonly recognized for being Microsoft’s default browser, but in more recent years it has gotten a bad reputation for being slow.

1995

First Cellphone On The Internet!

A Nokia 9000 Communicator in Finland is the first cellphone in the world to access the world wide web in 1996. However, this would not be commercialized before 1999, and this type access did not offer standard HTML.

1996

Opera Released

Opera sprouted from the Norwegian company Telenor, and was released in 1996. Opera never achieved the success of Internet Explorer or Firefox, but today, it does have about 1% of all usage shares, which is quite a lot when you think about it!

1996

Wi-Fi!

The first version of the "802.11 protocol" was released, and at the time, it provided about 2Mbit/s. A couple of years later, it was known as the Wi-Fi, which is what we use today. Wi-Fi uses radio waves to link computers up to the Internet. It's also known as Wireless Local Area Network (WLAN).

1997

Google

Google is founded by Larry Page and Sergey Bryn. Google in itself is a corporation, but also a website. The website offers services such as apps, satellite maps and email accounts, but Google is mostly known for its search-engine, which was the original concept in 1998. With this search-engine you can search the web for anything; videos, documents, websites and pictures. Google is the most visited website in the world and also the source of the web-browser; Google Chrome.

1998

Microsoft Mobile Explorer

As the mobile Internet developed, so did the browsers. Even though it wasn't really used by the general public until several years later, Microsoft worked hard to bring the web to the cellphone. for example, they bought "Hitchiker", and made it into their own, Microsoft Mobile Explorer. This browser could handle both HTML and WAP, and it was available on several of Sony's phones. So, even if it wasn't used that much, it was a breakthrough.

2003

Wikipedia

Wikipedia is launched by Jimmy Wales and Larry Sanger. It is one of the most popular websites in the world, and perhaps the most educational one. Wikipedia is an encyclopedia on the internet, with information on everything. It has more than 4.5 billion articles in English, and more than 18 billion page views. The only problem is that anyone can edit it (who is registered, of course), so there is always a small chance that what you’re reading is wrong.

2001

Tor Is Released

Tor (The Onion Router) is released in 2002. It is a software that keeps the identity and the location of their clients hidden from all surveillance and encrypts everything the user does, and because of that, it is also very slow. All of this is achieved through Tor’s own browser, Tor, which can also be used to access hidden information on the web, like the darknet.

2002

3G Arrives!

In 2002 3G was first launched in various European countries (It was launched in Japan in 2001). 3G is the "third generation of mobile telecommunications technology", and the 3G networks provide information on a transfer rate of at least 200 kbit/s, which is a lot faster than 1G and 2G. While the use of it was very limited at the time, this would eventually become the big thing in 2007.

2002

LinkedIn

LinkedIn is launched as a professional business-oriented social network site. It can be used to find jobs or to communicate between users. As of October 2014, it has about 300 million users.

2003

Safari Released

Apple’s Safari is released in 2003. Safari turned into the default for Macbook users, much like Microsoft’s Internet Explorer. Safari is known to be a fast browser (especially for Mac users), with good security and flexibility.

2003

Facebook

Facebook is founded by Mark Zuckerberg and his roommates at Harvard University. It is a social network used for communication and sharing between friends and family, which continued to grow exponentially after its founding. Today, Facebook has more than 1.3 billion active users, and has become a global phenomenon.

2004

Opera Mini

Opera Mini has several limited releases in Europe. It excelled at mobile Internet, mainly because of how versatile it was. This lead to it being the first browser to pass the "ACID2" test. The ACID2 test was designed to find flaws in the browser design, and so, this meant that Opera and Opera Mini were excellent for mobile browsing.

2004

Firefox Arrives

In 1998, Netscape launched the Mozilla Foundation, in response to Internet Explorer’s huge success. It got many names throughout the years, including Pheonix and Firebird, but eventually it turned into Mozilla Firefox, which was released in 2004. Firefox is known for good security, speedy browsing and its browser engine, Gecko. It quickly became popular and a competitor to Explorer.

2004

YouTube

Youtube is founded in 2005, and bought by Google in 2006. Youtube is a website that allows users to view, upload and share videos. The first video that was ever uploaded was this video, of a man visiting the zoo. Youtube is visited by more than 1 billion unique users every month, and about 100 hours of video is uploaded every hour. Needless to say, it is the biggest site for sharing videos on the Internet.

2005

Twitter

Twitter is created and launched. IT is another global phenomenon, much like Facebook. On Twitter, users send out small messages called “Tweets”, that people who are following them can see. These tweets are only a maximun of 140 characters long, so you don't have to worry about spamming. It’s an easy way to find out what your friends are up to, and much simpler to use than Facebook. As of July 2014, Twitter has more than 500 million users.

2006

The iPhone Is Launched

A big step towards mobile Internet was made with the release of the iPhone in 2007. Safari became iPhone's default browser, and it just became better and better, going from the use of 2G and 3G, to the use of 3G and 4G (like we use today). The iPhone allows for easy navigation with it`s touchscreen, although zooming might be required in some cases. The biggest problem is probably that it doesn't support Javascript, which limits the browsing.

2007

Google Joins The Browser War

Google Chrome was released by (you guessed it), Google, in 2008. Chrome was created with the Webkit layout engine, and most of its code is released as an open-source project called Chromium. It struggled slightly at first, but quickly became one of the most used browsers.

2008

The iPad

The iPad was launched in 2010 by Apple. The iPad is a tablet, which means that it is much bigger and allows for much easier navigating than the iPhone. This was a major breakthrough in mobile browsing as it made things so much easier. The iPad uses Safari as a default browser, just like the iPhone.

2010

The Current Browser War

Currently, the biggest competitors in the browser war are Internet Explorer, Mozilla Firefox and Google Chrome. Explorer peaked in 2002 and 2003, with about 95% of the browser usage share, but it has been declining ever since. Firefox has about 12-24%, and Chrome has about 45% of the usage share. Safari also deserves a mention, with 3.7%. Keep in mind though, that the user shares of browsers are incredibly difficult to calculate, so all of these numbers are estimates.
